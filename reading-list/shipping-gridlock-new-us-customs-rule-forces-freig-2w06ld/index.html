Shipping Gridlock: New US Customs Rule Forces Freight Forwarders to Reject E-Commerce Cargo

钛媒体 · 2026年7月1日

By | Yi En

NextFin News — "Starting July 8, 2026, all imported consumer goods regulated by the CPSC that require a CPC or GCC must submit core compliance data electronically to the ACE system before the cargo arrives at the port for customs clearance."

"Goods without this declaration face customs detention, clearance delays, or forced returns. The old model of clearing customs first and providing certificates later will no longer be supported."

As this deadline approaches, detailed policy warnings are flooding cross-border e-commerce groups. Panic over compliance is spreading. While most merchants want to comply, the industry currently lacks a standardized, clear blueprint for execution. This leaves many operators unsure how to proceed. At the same time, high testing and certification fees are squeezing small businesses and multi-SKU sellers.

The Red Line: Which Products Are Caught in the Dragnet?

"Our laboratory consulting lines are completely overwhelmed," an industry insider noted. "We are answering non-stop questions, processing test samples, and rushing out reports. We haven’t seen this level of activity in a long time."

The new mandate, known as CPSC eFiling, requires traditional paper Children’s Product Certificates (CPC) and General Certificates of Conformity (GCC) to be submitted as structured digital data through the US Customs Automated Commercial Environment (ACE) system. Customs will verify the data automatically before granting clearance.

The rule applies only to imports that already require a CPC or GCC. Importers can check a list of roughly 600 Harmonized Tariff Schedule (HTS) codes on the CPSC website to confirm their categories. Items designed for children aged 12 and under require a CPC, while adult consumer goods require a GCC.

CPC Product Categories (Children Aged 12 and Under)

  • Furniture: Cribs, children's desks, chairs, and wardrobes.

  • Electronics: Smart toys and children's smartwatches.

  • Apparel: Clothing, shoes, hats, underwear, pajamas, scarves, and gloves.

  • Toys: Plush toys, educational sets, building blocks, remote-controlled items, and puzzles.

  • Infant Care: Feeding bottles, pacifiers, strollers, walkers, and car seats.

GCC Product Categories (Adult General Consumer Goods under CPSC Scope)

  • Home Goods: Mattresses, carpets, bunk beds, adult furniture, and metal-wick candles.

  • Apparel & Textiles: Adult clothing, footwear, home textiles, and curtains.

  • Packaging & Materials: Child-resistant packaging, paints, coatings, and fireworks.

  • Tools & Equipment: Power mowers, portable gas containers, and multi-purpose lighters.

  • Batteries & Electronics: Lithium batteries, button cell batteries, chargers, and power banks.

The Shared-Bond Trap: Why Freight Forwarders Are Backing Out

The biggest logistical challenge is a strict new alignment rule. A freight forwarder named Xiao Zhang explained that after July 8, three separate entities must match 100%: the importer listed on the CPC certificate, the account registered in the CPSC online filing system, and the Importer of Record (IOR) listed on the actual customs entry.

If there is any mismatch, the automated ACE system will block the entry. This makes traditional shipping methods, where multiple small merchants share a freight forwarder’s customs bond, highly risky. If a shared bond is used, the certificate importer will not match the customs importer, causing an immediate system rejection.

"Children’s products now require sellers to provide their own IOR and customs bond for individual entry clearance," Xiao Zhang said. "For GCC items like adult apparel, sellers just provide the documents, and the forwarder handles the electronic filing."

Because a single non-compliant child's product can trigger a hold on an entire shared container, many logistics firms are simply refusing to accept CPC-regulated cargo.

"Even GCC items are being scrutinized. If a container holds just one mixed CPC item, the risk is too high," Xiao Zhang added. He has stopped taking children's products entirely because no one knows how strictly US Customs will enforce the rules on day one. Additionally, a large number of inactive customs bonds have recently been invalidated, signaling that widespread inspections are imminent.

For merchants who can still find shipping options, forwarders have raised the entry requirements. They demand that sellers have their own IOR and bond, effectively dismantling the traditional "all-inclusive" shipping tax models.

If a shipment is held, the financial penalties are steep. Port detentions are expected to last 45 to 60 days. Daily container storage and demurrage fees run between $150 and $300. A two-month delay can easily cost tens of thousands of dollars per container.

Voices from the Ground: Confusion and Caution

Many sellers are stuck in a wait-and-see pattern. Opinions on how to handle the shift vary widely across the community.

"There is no need to panic yet," one merchant argued. "Just get your inventory shipped before July 8. No one knows how strictly customs will actually check IOR data on day one. We will adjust our plans as we see how it plays out." Other sellers plan to route their shipments through Amazon Global Logistics (AGL) temporarily while waiting for clarity from the market.

For others, the constant policy shifts are causing significant stress. An apparel seller named Yezi expressed frustration after reading through the dense CPSC guidelines. "The rules are incredibly complex. It is very hard to tell which exact items need eFiling and which are exempt."

Yezi's business relies on selling clothing with multiple colors and sizes (SKUs). Testing agents informed him that running lab tests and filing data for every single variation will be extremely expensive. "Profit margins are already thin," Yezi said. "Every time an overseas policy changes, the compliance costs fall entirely on us."

Another clothing merchant pointed out that physical wash tags must now be completely remade. Regulations require these labels to permanently display the manufacturer name, US importer info, Registered Identification Number (RN), production date, and specific batch code. Missing information will lead to border holds.

Two major questions dominate seller forums: What happens if the CPC importer does not match the customs IOR? And how can a seller fulfill the US importer requirement without a physical US entity?

Survival Paths: Navigating the New Import Framework

An industry consultant named Hongxu offered practical advice on these exact issues. He noted that if a CPC importer does not match the customs IOR, merchants do not need to pay for new laboratory tests. The original test reports remain valid. The seller simply needs to re-issue the CPC document using the correct, matching IOR identity.

A valid CPC must include seven core pieces of information:

  1. Product identification details (Name, model, or ASIN).

  2. Cited safety regulations (e.g., ASTM F963, CPSIA Section 101).

  3. Issuer details (US importer or manufacturer name, address, and phone number).

  4. Manufacturing date and location (Month/year and city/country).

  5. Date of the most recent compliance test.

  6. Testing laboratory details (Name and address of a CPSC-approved lab).

  7. Record keeper contact information.

For overseas sellers without a US corporate entity, Hongxu outlined three compliance pathways to issue certificates:

  • Foreign Shipper as IOR: The exporter acts as the IOR from abroad and issues the CPC under their own name, though the certificate must still list a US compliance contact. This keeps costs low but carries higher compliance risks during electronic filing.

  • US Agent Service: Sellers hire a registered US agent to act as the official importer and issue the certificates. This aligns all three required data points for a recurring annual fee of a few thousand RMB.

  • Registering a US Company: This serves as a permanent solution for stable, long-term exporters. Initial registration costs between $500 and $1,500, with annual maintenance and tax filing fees ranging from $400 to $800. Improper management can result in local penalties and threaten import privileges.

Hongxu also clarified that sellers do not need to re-file electronic data every single time they switch freight forwarders or customs brokers. The eFiling system tracks the Importer of Record (IOR), not the broker. As long as the IOR and the product specifications remain identical, the pre-recorded data can be reused across different shipments.

The Cost Surge and Market Reshuffling

While small businesses scramble, large e-commerce brands with established US subsidiaries remain unaffected. A representative from a major cross-border brand confirmed that their domestic US entity easily handles the data matching requirements, shielding them from detention risks.

For smaller sellers, the contrast is stark. "I spent the last year learning corporate finance, tax laws, and export declarations," a seller named Haige shared. "Just as I got everything sorted, this new CPSC eFiling system hit. It makes me want to walk away entirely."

Haige noted that while European regulations used to be considered the most demanding, the US market is quickly raising its compliance barriers. Customs inspections are transitioning from random spot checks to automated, mandatory data reviews.

Logistics costs are reflecting this pressure. Merchants report that standard ocean freight rates rose by nearly 3 RMB per kilogram over a two-month span starting in May. Regulated categories under CPSC scope are facing even steeper shipping premiums.

Ultimately, the traditional model of relying on high-volume, low-cost distribution without regulatory oversight is coming to an end. While the rising thresholds are putting intense financial pressure on small businesses, some operators view the change as an opportunity.

"If the rules require a separate certification for every SKU, it will cost us a lot of money," one merchant admitted. "But it is an expense worth paying. A higher barrier to entry will naturally filter out the low-cost competition."
